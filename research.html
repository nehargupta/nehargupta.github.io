Low-income Housing Matching

This project started as a term paper in Duke's Economics 605, Advanced Microeconomics, advised by the wonderful Dr. Charlie Becker. I wrote this independent research paper in that course, which I plan to continue working on through Spring of 2020.
Abstract: Low-income households looking for federally funded housing benefits are eligible for one of two options. One is public housing, which would place them in a unit that is part of a complex maintained and owned by the government. The second is a housing voucher, which would provide a household with payments towards rent of a privately owned complex. Due to a large supply limitation, local public housing authorities, which distribute vouchers and public housing units, maintain waitlists for eligible households. The waitlists may be ranked by high priority households, but housing authorities do not incorporate household preferences. Households have heterogeneous preferences on housing options. They also can fail to find a house using a voucher. They may find minimally suitable housing but wish to keep searching. I discuss the importance of a joint mechanism of these two government programs and illustrate desirable properties of a such mechanism. Then, I illustrate with simulations the impact that relative preference for vouchers compared to public housing has on the utility of agents, as well as the impact an increasing cost of searching for a voucher rental unit has on utility of agents. This problem is distinct from the “dorm allocation” problem studied in the mechanism design literature because of two key features: excess demand, the stochastic arrival of the goods that need to be allocated, and a conceptually different type of housing option.

Bug Bounty Auctions

In Duke's CS 590: Computational Microeconomics, taught by Dr. Vince Conitzer, I had the chance to work with two amazing students: Elizabeth Margolin (Facebook, Privacy) and Alex Marsh (UNC Economics PhD Candidate) on a term paper examining corporate bug-bountry programs
In working on this project, we:
  • Estimated hackers’ incentives to sell security vulnerabilities based on their market perception
  • Built domain expertise in the marketplace of zero-day exploits on dark web economies
  • Explored impact to corporations, including features and products forced to change, and estimated cost to firms

Causal Inference Matching Algorithms

I'm currently working as an RA for the research team of Dr. Cynthia Rudin, Dr. Sudeepa Roy, and Dr. Alexander Volfovsky.
Currently, I'm writing an open-source Python package that implements and compares a variety of algorithms that match treatment and control units based on relevant covariates. This space will be updated when the code is available for public use. Currently, my code is available here. The algorithms under development is the implementation of these algorithms/papers: DAME and FLAME

Fairness in Toxicity Classification of Text

In Duke's CS 590: Privacy and Fairness in Data Science, taught by Dr. Ashwin Machanavajjhala and Dr. Brandon Fain, my team with Neil Pruthi and Yujing Ke examined the impact of biased training data on demographic fairness of supervised learning algorithms. We illustrated the relationship between human biases in text classification and success of online hate-speech filtering. A more detailed description of our project and code is available here: https://github.com/guptane6/cs590_privacy_fairness

This was my senior economics thesis at Michigan State University, advised by Dr. Jeff Biddle. I focused on the Affordable Care Act (“Obamacare”) and its state level implementations. I studied social media and sentiment analysis papers to incorporate the effect of sentiment on enrollment over time.
